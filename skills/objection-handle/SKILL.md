# Objection Handling

## Purpose
Turn a hesitation into a conversation — not a close. The goal isn't to overcome objections through pressure. It's to give the lead enough honest information to make a real decision.

## Core Principle
**Acknowledge → Understand → Reframe → Offer a path.**
Never skip straight to the pitch. A person who feels heard is 3x more likely to stay in the conversation.

## Objection Playbook

---

### Objection: "It's too expensive."

**Acknowledge:**
> "Yeah, pricing is always a real consideration — especially at your stage."

**Understand (ask one question):**
> "Is it that it doesn't fit the budget right now, or that you're not sure the ROI is there yet?"

**Reframe based on answer:**
- *Budget:* "We have a [free tier / startup plan / monthly option] that might make more sense as a starting point. Want me to walk you through what you'd get?"
- *ROI unclear:* "Fair — that's worth proving before you commit. Most teams see [specific outcome] within [timeframe]. I can show you a few examples if that'd help."

---

### Objection: "Not the right time."

**Acknowledge:**
> "Timing is real — I get it."

**Understand:**
> "Is it a bandwidth thing, or is there a specific milestone you're waiting to hit first?"

**Reframe:**
- *Bandwidth:* "This is actually designed to free up bandwidth, not add to it — but I don't want to pitch if the timing genuinely doesn't work. When would be better?"
- *Waiting for milestone:* "Makes sense. Want me to check back in with you around [their milestone]? I can set a reminder."

---

### Objection: "We're already using [Competitor]."

**Acknowledge:**
> "Good to know — [Competitor] is solid."

**Do NOT disparage the competitor.**

**Understand:**
> "Are you happy with it, or is there something it's not quite nailing for you?"

**Reframe if there's a gap:**
> "That gap — [specific thing they mentioned] — is actually exactly what we built [feature] for. Worth a 20-minute look?"

**If they're happy:** 
> "Totally fair. If that ever changes, we'll be here. Mind if I send you one thing we do differently — just so you have it?"

---

### Objection: "I need to check with my team / boss."

**Acknowledge:**
> "Of course — makes sense to loop them in."

**Offer to help the internal pitch:**
> "Want me to put together a quick summary you could share? Or if it's easier, I could hop on a call with both of you — sometimes that saves a few back-and-forths."

---

### Objection: "I'm not sure I trust this yet."

**Acknowledge:**
> "That's a reasonable place to be — we're still pretty new to you."

**Build trust, don't defend:**
> "What would make you more comfortable? Happy to share [customer stories / technical docs / a pilot option]."

---

### Objection: "We built something in-house."

**Acknowledge:**
> "That says a lot about your team — building it yourselves is a real commitment."

**Understand:**
> "Is it covering everything you need, or are there parts where it's not quite there?"

**Reframe only if there's a gap:**
> "The teams that switch from custom builds to us usually do it when [maintenance cost / feature gap / scale] becomes the bigger problem. If that's not you, honestly, keep doing what works."

---

## After Two "No" Signals

If the lead has declined twice in the same conversation:
1. Stop advancing the conversation
2. Close gracefully: *"Totally understood — I won't keep pushing. If things change, you know where to find us."*
3. Log as "disqualified" with reason

Never send a third pitch in the same session.

## Escalation
If the objection is about:
- **Legal or contractual terms** → escalate to founder
- **Security / compliance / data handling** → escalate to founder, do not speculate
- **A very large deal being blocked** → notify founder immediately

## Tone Notes
- Objections are information, not attacks. Stay curious, not defensive.
- Match their directness. A blunt objection deserves a blunt, honest response.
- Silence after an objection response is okay. Give them space.
